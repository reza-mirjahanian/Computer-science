The Sushi Principle

In data engineering, raw data is better than cooked data. Coined in 2015, this principle champions storing data in its original, unaltered state to preserve flexibility and maximum detail.

RAW DATA

🐟 The "Sushi" Approach

Like sushi, which is prized for its pure, unaltered ingredients, raw data is kept exactly as it arrived from the "golden source." It is not pre-aggregated, heavily filtered, or irreversibly transformed.

COOKED DATA

🍳 The "Cooked" Approach

"Cooked" data undergoes heavy ETL upfront. Once you aggregate or filter data early in the pipeline, you "cook" it. You can't un-cook an egg, and you can't un-aggregate a pre-summed dataset to find the original details.

Pipeline Architecture: Schema-on-Read vs. Schema-on-Write

✨ Why Raw is Better

  • Flexibility: New questions arise over time. Raw data lets you re-process for new use cases.
  • Traceability: You can always audit exactly what happened at the source.
  • Agility: Don't waste time designing complex transformations before you even know what questions you need to answer.

⚠️ The Catch (Data Swamps)

While the Sushi Principle advocates for raw storage, dumping raw data without governance creates a "Data Swamp." Raw data requires a strong "Schema-on-Read" approach, solid metadata cataloging, and strict access controls to be truly useful.

Originally coined by Bobby Johnson & Joseph Adler at Strata+Hadoop World (2015).
